To speak of one's self is always a challenge. The greatest of these is the underlying assumption of the task: that there is a self. And if there were a self, there would still be the task of what is consitutive of it. Is it the actions? The thoughts? A mixture of both? Does that mean, if the actions and the thoughts were to changeâ€”-an inevitability for anyone who goes about the business of living--that the self would change? But what, if anything, would maintain the continuity between these succeessive selves?

These questions are of the utmost importance to me, a convicted criminal. The charge to which I pled guilty was misdemeanor assault with a deadly weapon. I am neither proud nor ashamed of it. It is a thing I have done. I do not consider it to be a part of myself, as I believe anything that was done once cannot be rightly considered a part of an entire human being. But I still, of course, accept responsibility.

I am a thoughtful man, a cerebral man. For that reason I consider those very thoughts to be the constitutive parts of a person's character. And just as important is how a person goes about their thinking. Mine is a process that's methodical, cold, and distant. But it proceeds from a place of warmth. I do not, as others do, pretend that reason is the ruler of humanity. To paraphrase Hume, reason is, and ought to be, slave to the passions.
